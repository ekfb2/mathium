// LESSON 1 · SCATTER PLOTS

Scatter Plots & Correlation

A scatter plot shows the relationship between two variables by plotting points on a graph. The pattern of the points tells you the type of correlation.
01
Positive correlation — as one variable increases, the other increases. Points trend upward.
02
Negative correlation — as one increases, the other decreases. Points trend downward.
03
No correlation — no visible pattern. Points are scattered randomly.
04
An outlier is a point far from the main cluster. Note it but do not let it control your line.
POSITIVE → points go up-right | NEGATIVE → points go down-right | NONE → random scatter
// LESSON 2 · TRENDS & BEST FIT

Line or Curve of Best Fit

Once you identify a trend, draw a line or curve of best fit that follows the data. It should split the points roughly evenly above and below.
01
If the points form a straight trend, draw a line of best fit.
02
If the points form a curved trend, draw a curve of best fit.
03
The line should not connect every dot — it represents the overall trend only.
04
Balance points above and below the line equally when drawing it.
TREND: LINEAR → line of best fit | NON-LINEAR → curve of best fit
// LESSON 2 · INTERPOLATION & EXTRAPOLATION

Interpolation vs Extrapolation

Use your line or curve of best fit to predict values. The key difference is where the value falls relative to your data.
01
Interpolation — estimating a value inside the data range. More reliable.
02
Extrapolation — estimating a value outside the data range. Less reliable.
03
To interpolate: find your x-value on the axis, go up to the line, then read across to the y-axis.
04
Extrapolation requires extending your line beyond the data — always note this adds uncertainty.
INSIDE data range = INTERPOLATION | OUTSIDE data range = EXTRAPOLATION
// LESSON 5 · MEAN, MEDIAN, MODE

Measures of Central Tendency

Three ways to summarize a data set with one number. Each measures something different about the center.
01
Mean — add all values, divide by how many there are. mean = sum ÷ count
02
Median — order the data, find the middle. If two middle values, average them.
03
Mode — the value that appears most often. A data set can have more than one mode.
04
Range — max − min. Tells you the total spread of the data.
MEAN = Σx ÷ n | MEDIAN = middle value | MODE = most frequent | RANGE = max − min
// LESSON 5 · QUARTILES

Quartiles & IQR

Quartiles divide ordered data into four equal parts. They help describe where the middle of the data sits and how spread out it is.
01
Order the data from smallest to largest.
02
Q2 (Median) — the middle value of the full data set.
03
Q1 — the median of the lower half (values below Q2).
04
Q3 — the median of the upper half (values above Q2).
05
IQR — interquartile range = Q3 − Q1. Measures the middle 50% spread.
IQR = Q3 − Q1 | Q1 = median of lower half | Q3 = median of upper half
// LESSON 5 · BOX PLOTS

Box Plots Explained

A box and whisker plot is a visual summary of a data set using the five-number summary. It shows spread and center at a glance.
01
Find the five-number summary: Minimum, Q1, Median, Q3, Maximum.
02
Draw a number line. Place dots at Min, Q1, Median, Q3, and Max.
03
Draw a box from Q1 to Q3. Draw a vertical line at the Median inside the box.
04
Draw whiskers from Q1 out to Min and from Q3 out to Max.
BOX PLOT = Min | Q1 |—[ Q1 ··· Median ··· Q3 ]—| Max
